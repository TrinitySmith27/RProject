Introduction

In a political science course I took last semester, we learned that the two strongest correlates with US presidential election outcomes are 1) the state of the economy prior to the election, and 2) how long the current party in office has held power. In my project, I will seek to evaluate how these factors have correlated with election outcomes since 1960.

Data Description

The datasets used in this study are derived from two sources: The World Bank and CQPress. The World Bank provides the GDP per capita growth data, while CQPress offers election data, including the political party of each president and their time in office. The dataset contains these observations for each president since 1960 (party and time in office), along with the associated GDP growth for the election years.

It is worth noting that including both won_only_last_election and won_two_elections in the same analysis could lead to multicollinearity issues, as these variables are negatively correlated. By definition, if the incumbent party has won two or more elections, they cannot have won only the last election. Consequently, including both variables in this analysis results in a degree of skew in the conclusions.

While GDP growth does not appear to be a significant predictor, the number of consecutive terms the incumbent party has held office predictably influences electoral success.

Discussion

Based on the results from the main analyses, it can be concluded that while recent quantiative national economic performance has little to no effect on election results, previous election results have significant effects. The duration of a party’s incumbency is a substantial determining factor in election outcomes: having won just a single previous term is highly beneficial to incumbent parties, while having won two or more consecutively in the prior elections is harmful to their odds of success.

To further extend this study, it would be interesting to explore other factors that may influence election outcomes, such as social issues, international relations, and candidate characteristics.
